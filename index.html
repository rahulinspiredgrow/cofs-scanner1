Understanding Phytoplankton

Dive into the world of these microscopic marine organisms!

What are Phytoplankton?

Phytoplankton are tiny, photosynthetic organisms that drift in the upper layers of oceans, rivers, and lakes. They produce over 50% of the world's oxygen through photosynthesis and form the foundation of the aquatic food web. These microscopic plants, ranging from <2 µm to >2 mm, are critical for carbon cycling and supporting marine life. They are sensitive to environmental changes, serving as key indicators of ecosystem health.

Diatoms

Known for silica-based cell walls (frustules), e.g., Thalassiosira pseudonana.

Dinoflagellates

Feature two flagella, some bioluminescent, e.g., Noctiluca scintillans.

Cyanobacteria

Photosynthetic bacteria, often in freshwater, e.g., Microcystis aeruginosa.

Size-Based Classification

Size Class Size Range (ESD) Description
Picoplankton < 2 µm Smallest phytoplankton, often cyanobacteria, crucial for microbial loops.
Nanoplankton 2 - 20 µm Includes small diatoms and flagellates, key in nutrient cycling.
Microplankton 20 - 200 µm Larger diatoms and dinoflagellates, primary food for zooplankton.
Macroplankton 200 - 2000 µm Largest phytoplankton, like colonial forms, visible to the naked eye.
